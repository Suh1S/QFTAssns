\section{Analysis}

We perform data analysis by performing fits of the Ideal Shockley Diode equation on all the I-V characteristic scatter plots i.e. all I-V scatter plots of the semiconductor devices are fit with the ideal Shockley diode equation  $I = I_{s} \cdot (e^{\frac{qV}{\eta k T}} - 1)$, in the form of:

\vspace{1mm}

$I = m \cdot (e^{\frac{eV}{n k T}} - 1)$, where $m$ is $I_{s}$, $n$ is $\eta$, $q$ is $e$ and $\frac{kT}{e} \approx 25.86$ mV$^{-1}$ (at $300$ K). In a few cases, where offset by a constant is required, we use the equation:

\vspace{1mm}

$I = m \cdot (e^{\frac{V}{n \cdot 25.86}} - 1)  + k$, where $k$ is the offset constant.

\vspace{1mm}

We use this form of the Shockley diode equation and perform fits on all the I-V scatter plots. The errors obtained in the parameters are the intrinsic fit errors and not the measurement errors (as we can safely neglect measurement errors while performing the experiment; this makes error bars redundant). \textbf{This fitting is done under the assumption that $R_{r} = 0$.}

\vspace{2mm}


To find out $R_{r}$, we use a modified form of Shockley's diode equation i.e. 

\vspace{1mm}

$I = I_{s} \cdot (e^{\frac{q(V - I \cdot R_{r})}{\eta k T}} - 1)$

\vspace{1mm}

Now, we find $R_{r}$ (approximately) in the linear regime (where I-V have a linear relationship. \textbf{This happens at larger values of V}). Performing a first order Taylor expansion and making necessary approximations, we get:

\vspace{1mm}

$e^{\frac{q(V - I \cdot R_{r})}{\eta k T}} \approx 1 + \frac{q(V - I \cdot R_{r})}{\eta k T}$ (till first order)

\vspace{1mm}

Now setting $q$ as $e$ and simplifying, we get:

\vspace{1mm}

$I = I_{s} \cdot e \cdot \frac{(V - I\cdot R_{r})}{\eta k T}$ which gives us:

\vspace{1mm}

$I = \frac{\frac{e}{\eta k T}}{\frac{1}{I_{s}} + \frac{e\cdot R_{r}}{\eta k T}} \cdot V$, a linear relationship between $I$ and $V$.

\vspace{1mm}

Now, under the assumption (which is supported by order of magnitude of the values) that $\frac{1}{I_{s}} <<< \frac{e\cdot R_{r}}{\eta k T}$, we get:

\vspace{1mm}

$I \approx \frac{\frac{e}{\eta k T}}{\frac{e\cdot R_{r}}{\eta k T}} \cdot V = \frac{V}{R_{r}}$

\vspace{1mm}

Hence, the \textbf{reciprocal} of the slope ($\frac{\Delta I}{\Delta V}$) in the linear regime of the I-V characteristic curve for the semiconductor devices, yields the resistance, $R_{r}$. (as $R_{r} = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta I} = \frac{1}{\frac{\Delta I}{\Delta V}}$)

\vspace{3mm}

Knee voltage/turn-on voltage is calculated via eyeballing, from the I-V characteristic curve i.e. it is the voltage value at which $I \approx 5$ mA.

\vspace{3mm}

We now, begin fitting the I-V curves for all the semiconductor devices.
